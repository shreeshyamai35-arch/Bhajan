# Khatu Shyam Katha — The Story of Barbarika

Khatu Shyam, lovingly called Shyam Baba, is worshipped as a Kaliyuga form of
Lord Krishna. In traditional accounts drawn from the Skanda Purana and regional
folk tradition, he is identified with Barbarika, the brave son of Ghatotkacha
and grandson of Bhima, one of the Pandavas.

From childhood Barbarika was a mighty warrior and a sincere devotee. Through
penance he obtained three infallible arrows from the divine Mother, and a
powerful bow. With these "Teen Baan" he could win any battle, which is why he is
still praised as Teen Baan Dhari — the bearer of three arrows.

As the great war of Kurukshetra drew near, Barbarika vowed to his mother that he
would fight for whichever side was losing. Knowing that such a vow could turn the
war endlessly, Krishna came to him disguised as a Brahmin to test his strength
and his heart. When Krishna asked Barbarika to prove his power, the young warrior
showed that a single arrow could mark every leaf of a tree.

Recognising that Barbarika's power and his vow could overturn the cosmic balance
of the war, Krishna asked him for a gift — daan. Barbarika, true to the spirit of
selfless charity, offered his own head. Before his sacrifice he asked only to
witness the entire battle. Krishna granted the boon, placed his head upon a hill,
and from there Barbarika watched the whole of the Mahabharata war.

Pleased by this supreme act of devotion and sacrifice, Krishna blessed him: in
the age of Kali he would be worshipped by Krishna's own name, Shyam. Centuries
later, at the village of Khatu in Rajasthan, his sacred head is said to have been
discovered and enshrined. The temple at Khatu remains a great pilgrimage centre,
and devotees sing his praises as "Haare ka sahara" — the support of the defeated
and the helpless. Jai Shri Shyam.
